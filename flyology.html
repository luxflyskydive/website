Flyology

Why you float instead of fall.

The physics behind indoor skydiving — explained without a physics degree.

Lift vs gravity.

When air moves fast enough past a surface — including a human body — it creates an upward force called aerodynamic lift. In a vertical wind tunnel, the air column is moving upward at speeds between 130 and 350 km/h.

Your body acts as an airfoil: spread flat in an arch position, you generate enough lift to counteract your body weight. You don't float because the wind pushes you up — you float because your body shape creates lift from the airflow.

It's the same principle that keeps a plane in the sky. But instead of wings, you are the wing.

Body position effects

Body at 45° arch

High drag, high lift → stable hover

Body upright (sitting)

Less drag → need more wind speed

Body inverted (head-down)

Highest speed required, most efficient aerodynamically

Speed settings by discipline

Belly Fly (Arch)

130–160 km/h

The beginner position. Generates maximum drag, most stable, easiest to hold.

Sit Fly (Head Up)

180–220 km/h

Reduces your drag surface — you fall faster, need more wind, more control.

Head-Down

250–320 km/h

Maximum speed. The most challenging discipline. Requires coaching.

Dynamic (High-Speed)

280–350 km/h

Rapid transitions between positions. The most demanding discipline.

The four fundamental positions

Arch (Belly Fly)

Face down, arms and legs spread. The beginner position. Generates maximum drag, most stable, easiest to hold. Your entire front side becomes the wing.

Neutral (Delta)

Belly fly with arms slightly tucked. The intermediate control position for horizontal movement and turns. You're learning precision.

Sit Fly (Head Up)

Body vertical, seated position. Dramatically reduces your drag surface — you fall faster, need more wind, more control. A major progression milestone.

Head-Down

Inverted, head pointing toward the ground. Maximum speed. The most challenging discipline. Opens up advanced formations and speed work.

Why it gets easier.

The brain and body have to learn an entirely new relationship with gravity. In the beginning, you'll overcorrect, fight the air, and burn energy unnecessarily. As you accumulate tunnel time, your body learns to relax — and relaxed flying is efficient flying.

Session 1

Everything is new. You're learning to relax into the air, fighting the instinct to move. Exhausting but exhilarating.

Session 2

Noticeable improvement. Your body remembers what neutral feels like. You're starting to trust the air.

Sessions 3–5

Your nervous system is adapting. Movements become smoother. You can feel micro-corrections instead of fighting.

Sessions 10+

Movement is flowing. You're thinking about style and control, not survival. This is where learning accelerates.

Most flyers notice a significant improvement between their first and second session. The more you fly, the quieter your body becomes.

At head-down, your terminal velocity in freefall would be around 270 km/h. Our tunnel tops out at 350 km/h — enough to replicate it exactly.
